About Me

Hello, I'm Jeremy Dietrich! I'm the 2023 SESE Exploration Postdoctoral Fellow at Arizona State University, working on finding planets similar to the Earth around the nearest stellar systems. I received my PhD in 2023 from the University of Arizona working with Professor Dániel Apai, with my dissertation titled "Revealing Unseen Planets in Exoplanetary Systems". I received my undergraduate degree from Harvard University, graduating in 2017 with a joint concentration in Astrophysics and Physics with a secondary concentration in Statistics.

My research focuses on using the DYNAMITE software package I wrote to integrate the accurate yet incomplete data from any given exoplanet system with the population demographical data from Kepler and make statistical predictions on the parameters of the most likely additional undetected planet in the system. Using DYNAMITE and targeted follow-up observations, I will search the closest stellar systems for planets similar to the Earth in size and instellation, as well as determine what characteristics of a system are most correlated with a planet similar to Earth. For more, check out my Research.

Outside of my research, I am a large fan of baseball and am on a journey to see a game at all 30 MLB stadiums (currently at 22/30!). I also enjoy pub trivia, board games and role-playing games, and playing the guitar.

Research

In general, my research focuses on finding and studying exoplanet systems by combining observations with population statistics and demographics. My work is both theoretical and observational in nature, both predicting and looking for exoplanets in multi-planet systems as well as searching for them around the smallest and closest stars to us.

DYNAmical Multi-planet Injection TEster (DYNAMITE)

The search for and characterization of exoplanets in partially explored systems is still ongoing, and our understanding of the nature of planetary systems is incomplete. However, with the demographical data on exoplanet populations gained from the Kepler mission, we can begin to study these systems from a statistical perspective and determine the likelihood of the presence and orbital/physical parameters of additional planets in any system. To address this exciting area of research, I created the DYNAMITE software package, which combines the specific partial information from a given exoplanet system with population-level statistical knowledge to predict where these “hidden” additional planets can be found.

TESS

The Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS), which has been observing the whole sky since 2015, has found over 5,000 exoplanet candidates – a similar amount to the total confirmed number of exoplanets currently known. In 2020, I used DYNAMITE to predict where we would likely find additional planets in all the TESS multi-planet candidate systems known at the time (Dietrich and Apai 2020). Since then, TESS has continued to observe the night sky, and many focused follow-up observations have dramatically increased the number of known multi-planet systems, including finding more planets in the systems we studied. The 2020 DYNAMITE TESS predictions have been analyzed with this new data in hand and will be the focus of an upcoming paper, along with providing further and more precise predictions for the expanded suite of TESS multis.

Single System Analyses

There are many exoplanet systems that are interesting both due to their orbital architectures and their potential for further planetary characterization from current (JWST) and upcoming (GMT, HabEx/LUVOIR?) exoplanet studies, and I am using DYNAMITE to help categorize these systems. As an example, I studied the tau Ceti system of 4 known planets and 3 unconfirmed candidates and found support for all the unconfirmed candidates being real, along with a prediction for an additional planet candidate that orbits in the habitable zone of the star (Dietrich and Apai 2021). I am also working on more precise modeling of the dynamical stability for exoplanetary orbital architectures (Dietrich, Apai, and Malhotra 2022, and more to come!) and more accurate statistical distributions for many orbital and physical planetary parameters (Basant, Dietrich, and Apai 2022, and more to come!).

Exo-Earth Discovery and Exploration Network (EDEN)

In addition to studying planetary systems from a statistical standpoint, I am also interested in finding new planets and refining the known population even further. I was the lead data analyst and a major observer for the Project EDEN transit survey, an international project that for the past 4 years has completed a survey of the known single quiescent northern late M dwarfs (spectral types M7 - ~L0) within 15 pc and searching for exoplanets transiting these small nearby stars. Our results are the deepest photometric monitoring of this ensemble of stars and show no strong transit candidates (Dietrich et al. 2023).
